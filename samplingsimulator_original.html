The Central Limit Theorem: regardless of the population's shape, the sampling distribution of the mean approaches a normal distribution as n increases - provided the population has finite variance. This is why hypothesis tests and confidence intervals assume normality of sample means even when raw data is not normal. Try increasing n and re-running - watch the bell shape sharpen.

Standard Deviation (sigma)

The standard deviation of the population measures how spread out individual observations are around the population mean.

sigma = sqrt[ sum((x_i - mu)^2) / N ]
  • Property of the population, not any sample
  • Does not change as n increases
  • Answers: "How variable are individual values?"
  • Example: heights of all students have sigma = 3 in - fixed regardless of how many you measure

Standard Error of the Mean (SEM)

The SEM measures the spread of sample means around the population mean mu - how precise our estimate of mu is.

SEM = sigma / sqrt(n)
  • Property of the sampling distribution
  • Shrinks as n increases - bigger samples -> more precise estimates
  • Answers: "How much would my sample mean vary across repeated studies?"
  • Example: average height in samples of 100 has SEM = 3/sqrt(100) = 0.3 in

Sampling Distribution

Draw thousands of random samples of size n from the same population and plot all their means -> you get the sampling distribution of x-bar.

  • Center = population mean mu (unbiased estimator)
  • Spread = SEM = sigma/sqrt(n)
  • Shape -> normal as n grows (Central Limit Theorem)
  • Rarely constructed in practice - but underlies every hypothesis test and confidence interval

Why This Matters in Research

  • Confidence intervals use SEM, not sigma
  • t-statistics divide by SEM to standardize the estimate
  • Reporting sigma when you mean SEM misrepresents precision
  • A large sigma is fine - if n is large enough, SEM will be small and your estimate still precise
95% CI approx x-bar +/- 1.96 x SEM
