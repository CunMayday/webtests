Research Methodology Advisor

How the Decision Tool Works — A Guide for DBA Students

What This Tool Does

The Research Methodology Advisor helps DBA students choose a research methodology for an applied project. It asks a short series of practical questions about your goals, constraints, and data reality, then recommends:

  • One primary method best aligned with your answers
  • Up to two alternatives that could also work
  • Feasibility notes and cautions tailored to your constraints

The result also includes a brief overview of the recommended method—what it is, when to use it, advantages, considerations, examples, and next-step guidance. This tool converts your practical research situation into a clear, defensible methodology choice—with reasons, cautions, and next steps—so you can move from idea to execution with confidence.

How It Works

Step 1 — Answer a short sequence of questions

Questions cover your research purpose, level of control, time design, data sources, depth vs. generalizability, whether you’re building or evaluating, theory maturity, and feasibility.

Step 2 — The tool scores possible methods

Each answer adds points to methods that fit (and may add “watch-outs” when there’s a mismatch). For example: if you can randomize, experimental designs get a big boost; if you’re observational, correlational/case study gain points and experiments receive cautions.

Step 3 — It ranks the methods and delivers a recommendation

  • Primary recommendation = highest-scoring method (with reasons)
  • Alternatives = next-best 1–2 methods (with reasons)
  • Feasibility notes = guidance tied to your constraints

Step 4 — You get an explanation, not just a label

The output tells you why a method fits, what to watch for, and concrete next steps (e.g., power analysis for experiments; validated scales for surveys; sampling and coding plans for qualitative; integration plans for mixed methods).

What the Questions Cover (Eight Dimensions)

The advisor uses eight dimensions to triangulate the best methodological fit for your project.

1. Research Purpose

What core outcome must your research deliver for the organization?

Options: Explore • Explain • Predict • Improve

2. Level of Control

Can you manipulate the key variable and randomize assignment? This guides the choice among experimental, quasi-experimental, and observational/correlational designs.

Options: Randomized assignment • Manipulate without randomization • Observational only

3. Time Design

Will you observe change over time? This distinguishes snapshot studies from growth/change models and high-frequency forecasting.

Options: Cross-sectional • Longitudinal panel • High-frequency time-series

4. Primary Data Source

What data can you realistically obtain? This aligns methods with your actual access.

Options: Archival/organizational data • New survey • Qualitative (interviews/observation/docs) • Mixed sources

5. Depth vs. Generalizability

Is your priority rich contextual understanding or findings that scale across populations? This balances qualitative depth with quantitative breadth.

Options: Contextual depth • Generalizable estimates • Both

6. Innovation vs. Evaluation (shown only when “Improve” is your purpose)

Are you building something new, co-creating change with stakeholders, or evaluating an existing intervention? This routes you to design science, action research, or quasi-experimental/survey evaluation.

Options: Build a new artifact • Co-create change • Evaluate existing intervention

7. Theoretical Maturity

How well-developed is the theory base in this area? This steers toward exploratory/qualitative methods for thin theory or confirmatory/quantitative methods for mature theory.

Options: Thin (build/refine frameworks) • Mature (test/extend)

8. Feasibility Guardrails

What constraints apply around resources, access, ethics, and timeline? Significant constraints may upweight leaner designs such as correlational studies, case studies, or systematic reviews.

Options: Strong support • Some constraints • Significant constraints

Which Methods the Tool Can Recommend

Depending on your answers, the advisor selects from the following fifteen research approaches:

What the Final Answer Is Based On

Weighted fit: Your responses increase or decrease scores for each method according to methodological logic (e.g., randomization favors experiments; mixed sources favor mixed methods; high-frequency data favors time-series).

Reasons & cautions: Each recommendation lists why it fits and any watch-outs (e.g., sample size, integration workload, ethics, access).

Feasibility guidance: Notes reflect your constraint level (e.g., suggesting staged approaches or synthesis when primary data are hard to obtain).

Transparency: You see a full path summary (your choices), the primary method, alternatives, and next steps tailored to your selection—so you can always explain and defend how you arrived at your methodology.

How to Use the Output

  1. Check the rationale. Does the “why this fits” match your real constraints and objectives? If not, revisit your answers.
  2. Scan cautions early. Address feasibility risks before committing to a design (e.g., power analysis, access letters, IRB approval, stakeholder time).
  3. Plan your next steps. Use the listed actions to set up instruments, sampling, protocols, or integration plans.
  4. Consider an alternative. If the primary method is too demanding for your timeline, the alternatives offer viable Plan B or Plan C options.

Limits to Keep in Mind

  • It is a decision aid, not a substitute for advisor approval. Always discuss your final methodology selection with your dissertation committee or faculty advisor.
  • Recommendations assume honest inputs about your access, timeline, and skill set. Overstating capabilities leads to poor fits.
  • Complex projects may need phased designs (e.g., start correlational, move to quasi-experimental, then integrate qualitative). The tool may not capture every nuance of a multi-phase plan.
